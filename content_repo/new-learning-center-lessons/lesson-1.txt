TITLE=ACHIEVING SUCCESS IN TELEPATHY

[center]**Achieving Success In Telepathy**[/center]


          Telepathy is the transfer of information from one mind to another. Reports of telepathic experiences appear throughout history and across many cultures. Many believe that every human being has some telepathic skill. Gemetics appears to play a role, since native ESP ability has been observed to run in families. If everyone has at least some ability, it seems likely that anyone who works seriously at improving telepathic skill will experience increasing success over time.

           This training system was developed to optimize the conditions under which telepathic ability can be practiced and strengthened. Performance is monitored using established statistical methods. It uses a synchronized countdown that both sender and receiver share in real time, wherever they may be in the world: Three... two... one... and an image is presented to the sender. Detecting telepathic information is easier when the receiver knows exactly when the sender begins focusing on and transmitting that information.

          When the countdown ends on the receiver's screen, the screen goes blank and a short beep sounds. The receiver then typically closes their eyes and focuses attention on the contents of the mind's eye. What newly appears there, as if out of nowhere, at about the moment the countdown ends? This is the perceptual task the receiver must work to develop.

          When visual information is perceived at that time, it is generally low in contrast, unclear, and fleeting. Sometimes color is present. A few visual features can often be picked out, along with their relative size and position in the visual field, for example on the left side or the right side. Even a small amount of accurate visual feature information can be enough to distinguish the target image, the image viewed by the sender, from one or more decoy images with different visual features.

          The receiver must do more than notice features that appear fleetingly in their mind's eye. The receiver must also remember the shape or shapes, the color or colors, and the location or locations of one or more of the disconnected forms that were perceived. Much of this often happens within the first few seconds after the countdown ends. This is the moment when the visual information is new for the sender. Brainwave research has shown that newly presented visual information tends to produce a strong transient burst of brain activity. Because the information is new for the sender, it would also be new for the receiver. That is the right time to attend to it, since when information  generates a stronger response in the sender it also tends to be received more strongly.

          When we think about telepathy logically, we are tempted to conclude that it should be impossible. How can any signal not degrade with distance? Yet repeated experiments suggest that telepathic information is not measurably diminished by distance or by known intervening obstacles, including mountains, oceans, metal shielding, and the earth itself. How can something as subtle as a thought in one person's mind be conveyed across thousands of miles in real time? And how can one person tune in effortlessly to the thoughts of a single other person anywhere on earth while billions of other minds are active at the same time? Yet this exactly what happens. The connection between sender and receiver is established unconsciously, automatically, and without effort.

          It is important to realize that the fact that something cannot yet be explained by current science does not mean that it cannot exist. Belief is stubborn. It took many years for intelligent physicians to adopt handwashing before delivering babies. Many resisted because they could not accept that invisible germs were real and could carry disease, even after Ignaz Semmelweis showed that this simple practice greatly reduced sickness and death in mothers after childbirth.

          A receiver needs to fully believe that telepathy exists and fully trust that the information is definitely available in their mind's eye right after the countdown. It has been shown that those who don't believe that telepathy exists are much less likely to experience it. However, it has also been shown that a sender does not need to believe in telepathy in order to send telepathic information competently.

          A receiver has greater success when their state of mind is calm rather than agitated while attempting to receive visual telepathic information. If a receiver is overly concerned about getting good results, or experiences performance anxiety, this can significantly reduce the ability to receive effectively. A more playful or detached attitude is better. The less anxiety one brings into the task, the better one is often able to perform. This type of relaxed "don't care" attitude takes time to develop. With repeated familiarity, the experience of receiving telepathic information becomes less startling and less unusual, and more like just another successful act of perception.

          A final thought: many people, when first considering telepathy, are willing to believe that it may be real for some others, yet still feel strongly that they themselves do not have the sensitivity needed to receive telepathic information. They may think they could serve as a sender, but not as a successful receiver. Yet when some of these same people actually give receiving a fair try, they are surprised to discover that they really can be telepathic. The advice to those who assume they are incapable of receiving is simple: get over it.
